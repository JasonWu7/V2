Mental Health Disorders and Depression Data Statistics
Mental Health Disorders and Depression are critical aspects of the human experience that significantly impact individuals' lives, affecting their emotional and psychological well-being. These conditions encompass a wide range of mental health challenges, each with its unique characteristics and effects. Understanding these issues is essential to provide support, reduce stigma, and improve the overall mental health of individuals and communities.

The visualized data is sourced from Kaggle and presents data on patients with mental health issues from different countries from 1990 to 2017.

As of 2017, the global population affected by mental health issues reached a staggering 801,321,216 people, and mental health problems are prevalent in countries worldwide.

Sweden, Germany, and Finland are three countries that prioritize mental health issues, allocating a higher proportion of their national budgets to mental health care. However, this doesn't necessarily mean they have the fewest mental health patients. On the contrary, their emphasis on mental health likely results in more accurate diagnosis and reporting of mental health patients, which may lead to slightly higher data.

In contrast, China lags behind in mental health education, outreach, and funding compared to many other countries, potentially resulting in data that underestimates the actual values.
Environmental factors can also impact mental health, for example, Greenland experiences several months of polar day and polar night each year. This significantly affects people's circadian rhythms, daily routines, and living conditions, making it a major contributing factor to the high prevalence of mental health disorders among the local population.

The global number of individuals with mental disorders amounts to 10.6% of the world's total population, meaning that one in every ten people has experienced or is currently experiencing challenges related to mental health.

In extensive research within the field of mental health, a common phenomenon emerges: women are more prone to mental health issues than men. It is attributed to complex factors, such as biological elements, differences in the physiological makeup of males and females, the impact of hormonal fluctuations on emotions and mental health. For instance, psychological factors play a role, as women tend to be more emotionally oriented, and social factors, such as gender inequality in certain regions, also contribute to this trend.

Comparison
The chart on the left displays the top ten countries with the highest number of mental health issue patients, standardized for comparison.

Upon comparison, there is not a significant difference in the number of patients between most neighboring countries. The overall data does not exhibit striking extremes to suggest that certain countries have a significantly higher number of patients.

When looking at the distribution, patients with different categories of mental disorders show slightly different patterns, with most following a very similar linear or exponential distribution.

Overall, the distribution of patients globally is relatively uniform. This implies that mental health issues have a certain universality across different cultures and geographical backgrounds, and they are not confined by national borders, capable of occurring on a global scale.
When we look at the global total numbers of patients with different categories of mental health issues on the right bar chart, it appears to follow a distribution similar to the one above, resembling an exponential distribution. This implies that the majority of patients with mental health issues are primarily affected by a few specific mental health problems.

Anxiety Disorders and Depression patients make up the majority, not only because they are extremely common mental disorders themselves, but also because they encompass a wide range of issues such as social anxiety, separation anxiety, panic, feelings of helplessness, and depression, among others.

In contrast, there are fewer patients with Schizophrenia and Eating Disorders. This is partly because they specifically describe particular symptoms, resulting in a narrower range of coverage. Schizophrenia has a relatively lower incidence compared to other categories, yet it is one of the most severe mental health issues, causing patients to experience hallucinations, delusions, and even impacting their lifespan directly. Eating disorders mainly manifest in terms of eating behaviors, such as anorexia or binge eating. The lower number of patients in the data is partly due to the fact that their symptoms may not receive adequate attention, and this issue often occurs in adolescents or children, with parents attributing it to picky eating or growth phases.
Although certain categories may involve more severe symptoms, no mental health problem, no matter how small, should be ignored. They can impair concentration, induce feelings of pessimism, negativity, and even lead to suicidal tendencies. They can also have a direct impact on physical health.
